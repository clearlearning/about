Mentorship in the Regulatory Profession

Cross-Jurisdictional Connection

Mentorship within the CLEAR community connects professionals who work in regulation but in different organizations, jurisdictions, and regulatory structures. Unlike internal mentoring, this program focuses on sharing practical regulatory insight across agencies, boards, and jurisdictions.

Unlike internal mentoring programs within a single agency, the Elevate program focuses on sharing practical regulatory insight across agencies, boards, and jurisdictions within the global regulatory community.

Areas of Discussion

Mentors and mentees are encouraged to discuss how their organizations approach regulatory challenges such as investigations, board governance, stakeholder engagement, policy development, and protecting the public interest.

What You'll Gain

These conversations allow participants to:

  • Compare regulatory approaches across jurisdictions
  • Learn how different boards or agencies structure their work
  • Discuss real-world regulatory challenges
  • Build professional relationships across the global regulatory community

Goal Setting

SMART Goal Setting

Mentorship goals in the Elevate program should focus on strengthening regulatory leadership, decision making, and professional influence within regulatory environments.

Examples of regulatory mentorship goals include:

Developing stronger investigative interviewing techniques for regulatory complaints
Learning how other jurisdictions manage complex disciplinary cases
Building skills for working effectively with a regulatory board or council
Understanding how different regulators approach risk based regulation
Expanding professional networks across regulatory organizations
Preparing for leadership roles such as Director, Registrar, or Executive Director

SMART is an acronym that stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-based. Each element of the SMART framework works together to create a goal that is carefully planned, clear, and trackable.

You may have set goals in the past that were difficult to achieve because they were too vague, aggressive, or poorly framed. Working toward a poorly-crafted goal can feel daunting and unachievable. Creating SMART goals can help solve these problems. Whether you're setting personal or professional goals, using the SMART goal framework can establish a strong foundation for achieving success.

Click on the tabs below to see how we turn a goal like "I want to be in leadership" into a SMART goal.

SMART Goals

Select a letter to explore each element of the SMART framework.

S = Specific

Be as clear and specific as possible with what you want to achieve. The more narrow your goal, the more you'll understand the steps necessary to achieve it.

💡 Example

"Improve my ability to present investigation findings to a regulatory board."

M = Measurable

What evidence will prove you're making progress toward your goal?

Setting milestones along the way will give you the opportunity to re-evaluate and course-correct as needed. When you achieve your milestones, remember to reward yourself in small but meaningful ways.

💡 Example

"Review two completed cases with my mentor and practice preparing board level briefing summaries."

A = Achievable

Have you set an achievable goal?

Setting goals you can reasonably accomplish within a certain timeframe will help keep you motivated and focused.

Before you begin working toward a goal, decide whether it’s something you can achieve now or whether there are additional preliminary steps you should take to become better prepared.

💡 Example

"Use real examples from my current investigations."

R = Relevant

When setting goals for yourself, consider whether or not they are relevant. Each of your goals should align with your values and larger, long-term goals.

Ask yourself why the goal is important to you, how achieving it will help you, and how it will contribute toward your long-term goals.

💡 Example

"Strong case presentation is essential for regulatory decision making."

T = Time-Based

Is your goal time-bound? An end date can help provide motivation and help you prioritize.

For example, if your goal is to earn a promotion to a more senior position, you might give yourself six months. If you haven't achieved your goal in that timeframe, take time to consider why. Your timeframe might have been unrealistic, you might have run into unexpected roadblocks, or your goal might have been unachievable.

💡 Example

"Complete this work within the first three months of the mentorship program."

Roles and Expectations

Defining and understanding your role in a mentor program can help to ensure the best possible mentorship experience.

Mentoring Roles

Select a role to explore responsibilities and expectations.

The Mentee's Role

As a mentee, it is your job to absorb as much information from your mentor as possible. You will take on certain roles that are essential to the mentoring relationship:

  • Planner: Take the initiative to schedule meetings and create action plans. Be proactive about identifying and setting goals and timeframes.
  • Investigator: Probe and ask open-ended questions to get the most out of your conversations. Follow up frequently and continually provide updates.
  • Student: As a continuous learner, absorb knowledge from your mentor and take initiative to continue your learning outside of the mentoring relationship.

What is Expected of the Mentee?

  • Follow through on agreements: When action points have been identified during meetings, it is important that you follow through. Be accountable and proactive on set agreements.
  • Feedback: Provide feedback and be open to receiving feedback. Welcome constructive feedback, and use it as an opportunity for improvement.

The Mentor's Role

As a mentor, it is your primary role to support and guide your mentee based on their unique developmental needs. Throughout the relationship, you will wear different hats:

  • Coach/Advisor: Provide guidance, feedback, and advice by sharing your own experiences and insights. Provide input into the mentoring action plan.
  • Devil's Advocate: Deliver feedback that will challenge and help the mentee think through important decisions. Push them to take risks when appropriate and consider the potential consequences of decisions and actions.
  • Resource Person: Identify resources for personal and professional development. Expand the mentee's network of contacts by introducing them to new contacts or encouraging them to attend networking events.
  • Support System/Cheerleader: Encourage your mentee to try new things and move out of their comfort zone. Celebrate their wins and motivate them through setbacks.

What is Expected from the Mentor?

  • Be available: While not 24/7, be accessible to your mentee, especially at crucial times.
  • Withhold judgment: Build trust so they can share their thoughts and dilemmas with you. Meet them where they are without imposing your own goals and values too heavily.
  • Trustworthy: Stick to your promises, hold accountability, and maintain the utmost professionalism. What the mentee shares should be kept confidential.

Shared Expectations for All Participants

Our expectations for program participants regardless of their role:

  • Prepare for your meetings.
  • Arrive on time.
  • When life happens, communicate!
  • Complete and follow through with action items.
  • Set and share appropriate boundaries with your connection.
  • Abide by organization policies for appropriate workplace conduct.

Developing Meaningful Connections

The first meeting should focus on understanding each participant’s regulatory background and the context in which they work.

  • What profession or sector does your organization regulate?
  • What is the governance structure of your regulatory body?
  • What are the most complex challenges your organization currently faces?
  • What type of regulatory work do you personally handle (investigations, licensing, policy, governance, compliance, etc.)?
  • What leadership or professional skills would you like to develop through this mentorship?

Because participants work in different jurisdictions, discussing differences in legislation, governance models, and regulatory authority can be particularly valuable. This initial conversation will set the stage for what the mentee wishes to get out of the experience and will guide the conversations going forward.

Prepare ►

The following questions are designed for internal reflection before meeting with your connection for the first time:

  • Define your own expectations, goals, and what you want out of the connection.
  • What does my connection need to know about me?
  • What should I know about them?
  • Consider how much time you can commit to mentoring. Ensure that your availability aligns with the expectations and goals of your connection.
Establish Rapport ►

Because mentors and mentees come from different organizations, building trust and professional rapport is critical.

Consider discussing:

  • What motivated you to work in the regulatory field?
  • What regulatory issues are currently shaping your profession or sector?
  • What lessons have you learned from difficult regulatory decisions?
  • What leadership qualities are most important in regulatory environments?
Set Expectations ►

Conflict is simply a gap in expectations. Communicating and setting these expectations in the beginning of your connection could negate conflict and lead to increased satisfaction throughout your program.

  • What do you expect from a mentee or mentor?
  • What are the key objectives you wish to achieve?
  • When and how will we interact?
  • Who will initiate these interactions?
  • How do you approach conflict?
  • How do you best receive feedback?
  • What would make you want to terminate the mentoring relationship before the program end date?
Follow Through ►

An easy way to establish trust is to follow through on the action items that you agree to. If you say you will schedule an appointment, be sure to do so. Here are some tips to make sure nothing slips through the cracks.

  • Take notes
  • Schedule your next meeting
  • Set reminders

Effective Mentoring Session

ce Breaker Questions

Here are a few suggested topics for Ice Breaker questions

  • What is the most unusual or surprising issue you’ve encountered in your regulatory work?
  • If you could regulate one thing in everyday life (for example: airline boarding, group emails, meetings), what would it be?
  • What is one regulatory topic or issue you find particularly interesting right now?
  • What’s something about your regulatory system or jurisdiction that others might find surprising?
  • What is one professional skill you’ve developed in regulation that you didn’t expect when you started?
  • If you could instantly become an expert in one regulatory topic, what would it be?

Mentorship Meeting Agendas

Items to include in your Mentorship Meeting Agenda:

  • Ice Breaker question
  • Discussion of a current regulatory topic or challenge
  • Discussing action items
  • Reflection on professional development goals
  • Identification of next steps before the next meeting

Prompts for Next Steps

  • What items should you send your mentee?
  • What items should they send back to you?
  • Do you have a specific deadline or is everything due prior to your next meeting?
  • Schedule for your next meeting.

Identifying Gaps and Connecting to Resources

Mentors can often provide insight based on lessons learned from their own regulatory careers.

For example: mentors may share experiences related to complex investigations, board governance challenges, legislative change, or implementing new regulatory processes.

Here are a few questions to consider:

What do I wish someone told me when I started in this field?
What are some recurring problems, or gaps, that I am seeing with new hires, or with employees in general?
Are there repetitive questions or concerns that keep coming up? If so, how can I close this gap in a more efficient way?
What resources are available within the organization?

Concluding Mentorship Connections

Before meeting with your connection for your final meeting, here are some questions for you to reflect on.

Where did we begin?
How far have we come?
What in this program helped you?
What skills did you develop?
What skills did you see growth and development in your connection?

You can determine which areas you want to share with your connection. Some growth through this program could be personal. Depending on your relationship with your connection, you can decide which answers are relevant for them.

Finally, when expressing gratitude to your mentor or mentee, be specific with areas in which they had impact. This feedback will help them identify their strengths and continue to grow and develop their skillsets.

Challenges in Mentoring

Time Management

Understanding Your Priorities

Because participants work in demanding regulatory environments, time management and competing priorities may affect scheduling. Understanding your priorities will help you organize and deal with them in order of importance. The Eisenhower Matrix is a great tool to categorize your overwhelming to-do list into four categories. Those tasks categorized as "Important and Urgent" will be on the top of your priority list, while "Not Important and Not Urgent" tasks will be placed at the bottom.

Click each quadrant to learn more.

Progress: 0 of 4 explored

Important & Urgent — Do It Now

These are tasks that require your immediate attention. They are critical to your goals, have pressing deadlines, and cannot be postponed.

Examples

  • Crisis situations or emergencies
  • Pressing deadlines for key deliverables
  • Urgent requests from leadership
  • Time-sensitive client or partner issues

Important but Not Urgent — Schedule It

These tasks are significant for long-term success but do not require immediate action. Schedule dedicated time for them to prevent them from becoming urgent.

Examples

  • Strategic planning and goal setting
  • Professional development and training
  • Relationship building and networking
  • Mentorship meetings and preparation

Not Important but Urgent — Delegate It

These tasks feel pressing but don't contribute directly to your goals. Where possible, delegate them to others or handle them quickly without investing significant effort.

Examples

  • Certain emails and messages requiring quick replies
  • Meeting requests that could be handled by others
  • Routine administrative tasks
  • Interruptions and ad-hoc requests

Not Important & Not Urgent — Eliminate It

These tasks are distractions that don't contribute to your goals or require timely action. Minimize or eliminate them to reclaim your time.

Examples

  • Excessive social media browsing
  • Unnecessary meetings with no clear purpose
  • Busywork that doesn't move the needle
  • Over-organizing or perfectionism on low-priority items

Unresponsive Mentees

One of the most common challenges in mentorship connections is that mentees become inactive. Here are some tips and tricks to elicit response and reengagement within the program.

Mentees can go unresponsive for any number of reasons. Most common we have seen include:

  • Bandwidth
  • Time of Year
  • Approaching Deadlines
  • Personal Concerns
  • Mentorship Connection

Steps to Re-Engage

If a mentee is not responding to your initial contact or has not reached out to you to schedule a meeting, here are a few steps you can follow to address this concern.

Step 1: Check Communication Preferences

Make sure you are reaching out via the mentee's preferred communication channel as indicated in their profile. If they prefer email, send an email. If they prefer Teams or Slack, reach out there. As a last resort, try LinkedIn.

Step 2: Follow Up

Follow up in a timeframe approved by your program administrator. Qooper recommends following up one week after the initial contact.

Tips to Encourage Re-Engagement

Try to pull from your first meeting with your mentee to encourage meaningful conversations:

Ask Questions

  • Is there an end in sight to your project?
  • Would you like to schedule a meeting after your deadline?
  • How can I support you as your mentor in this hectic time?

Encourage & Support

"I know you are working so hard on XYZ project. You're doing important work."

Spark Interest

"I remember you were really excited about XYZ. Let's schedule a meeting to discuss this further."

Unrealistic Expectations

Unrealistic expectations can be tricky to manage. The key is open communication and willingness to listen and compromise.

Expectations may seem realistic in the moment. It may not be apparent that expectations are in place until they are left unmet. In every day life, we see that various factors out of our control can affect our plans, ideas, and expectations. Work and mentorship connections are not exempt to these factors and nuances.

This training will help you ask questions and create a game plan to identify and redirect unrealistic expectations.

A Game Plan for Realistic Expectations

1. Share the Goal and Mission

Share the goal and mission of the program with your connection so you're both aligned from the start.

2. Be Honest About Your Limitations

Be honest and authentic about your limitations in terms of time, knowledge, and network. Transparency builds trust.

3. Set Intentions for Reply Times

Agree on realistic response times so neither party is left wondering or feeling ignored.

4. Remember That You Are Human

It may seem possible to do something, but in reality it may take longer than you think or require more energy or resources. Extend grace and be sure to have open and honest communication with your mentees.

Providing Feedback

For those who fear conflict and confrontation, feedback can be intimidating. This lesson will help you shift your focus to the opportunity that comes through providing and receiving feedback.

Remember to focus on providing tips and advice rather than a full on evaluation.

Some questions you can ask or use to provide feedback include:

If you were to handle this situation, how would you have done it differently?
What is one thing I can do better next time?
What are other possible solutions that I am missing?

Compassion in Regulatory Complaints ►

Additional Information

Compassion is a fundamental element throughout the complaints process against a regulated professional. It's imperative to recognize that both the complainant and the licensee are individuals with unique experiences and emotions. The complainant, who may have experienced harm or dissatisfaction, requires support as they navigate the often daunting process of seeking redress. Their concerns should be met with empathy and understanding, as they may already be feeling vulnerable or marginalized. Similarly, the licensee facing allegations must also be treated with compassion. While accountability is essential, it's equally important to remember that they too are human, subject to error and stress. A compassionate approach acknowledges their perspective, offering support and guidance as they engage with the process. Ultimately, a compassionate approach fosters a more equitable and respectful complaints process.

Join us to hear real-world examples from regulatory organizations that have incorporated the element of compassion into their complaints process. Shilo Tooze, Deputy Registrar at the College of Veterinarians of Ontario, will discuss how the College of Veterinarians of Ontario focused on changes to increase compassion in the complaints process when legislation was creating a barrier to improve the process. Shilo will share strategies that have been used to increase the satisfaction of individuals involved in the complaints process and have led to a reduction in the number of complaints filed. Micah Matthews, Deputy Executive Director of the Washington Medical Commission will share two programs. One is aimed at reducing the need for a full investigation utilizing expert review and the other is designed to encourage voluntary self-education to mitigate future complaints. Matthew Hardy, National Director Notifications with Ahpra, will talk about how the Australian health practitioner regulator is responding to its Expert Advisory Group’s findings on minimising practitioner distress in several facets of its complaints management processes.

AI and Implications for Regulation of Professional Work ►

Session Description

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is increasingly prominent in professional work, yet regulation to ensure its safe and effective implementation into daily practice has lagged technological innovation. Ensuring proportionate regulation to support responsible adoption of AI by professionals requires balancing of risks without stifling innovation, in the context of technologies that few professionals themselves understand. This session will provide opportunities to reflect upon and discuss principles to guide regulators in considering whether or how AI can or should be regulated in professional work.

Speaker Bio

Zubin Austin, BScPhm, MBA, MISc, PhD, FCAHS | Professor and Koffler Research Chair | University of Toronto

Zubin Austin is a Professor and the Koffler Research Chair at the Leslie Dan Faculty of Pharmacy, University of Toronto. His research focuses on the professional and personal development of the health workforce. He has published over 250 peer-reviewed papers and authored five textbooks.

In 2017, in recognition of his work's global impact, he was installed as a Fellow of the Canadian Academy of Health Sciences, the highest honour for health researchers in Canada. He is also the only University of Toronto professor to have received both the President’s Teaching Award for sustained excellence as an educator and the President’s Research Impact Award for the societal significance of his work. Students named him Professor of the Year on 20 separate occasions.

Crisis Management for Regulators ►

Session Description

In times of crisis, how regulators communicate can be as important as the decisions they make. This one-hour virtual workshop explores the critical role of openness, transparency, and clear messaging in protecting the public interest. Through a facilitated approach and case studies, participants will examine how effective crisis communications not only manage immediate challenges but also strengthen long-term trust in regulatory bodies. The session highlights strategies that help regulatory leaders and staff become stronger communicators under pressure, ensuring the public receives timely, accurate, and meaningful information when it matters most.
